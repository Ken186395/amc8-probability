Lesson 1 ¬∑ Core Idea

What Is Probability? ¬∑ Candy Box Story

Goal: Understand ‚ÄúProbability = number of favorable outcomes √∑ number of equally likely outcomes‚Äù. Start using fractions to talk about chance.
Equally likely Fractions Sample space

1. Story: The Candy Box Game

Mom has 3 identical candy boxes. Only 1 box has a chocolate inside.
Lynne closes her eyes and picks one box at random.
Question: How big is her chance to get the chocolate?

Because all 3 boxes look the same and are chosen randomly, each one is equally likely to be picked.

Box 1: üç´ chocolate ‚Üê favorable outcome (1) Box 2: ‚ùå empty Box 3: ‚ùå empty Total possible outcomes: 3 Favorable outcomes: 1 P(get chocolate) = 1 √∑ 3 = 1/3

2. Three pieces of vocabulary

  • Experiment: Something random you do (pick a box, roll a die, flip a coin).
  • Outcome: What actually happens (picked box 1, rolled a 4, etc.).
  • Sample space: The list of all possible outcomes.

When all outcomes are equally likely:

Probability = (number of favorable outcomes) √∑ (number of equally likely outcomes)

3. Try it

  1. A bag has 5 candies: 2 red, 3 blue.
    (a) What is the probability of drawing a red candy?
    (b) What is the probability of drawing a blue candy?
  2. A fair die has faces 1‚Äì6, all equally likely.
    (a) Probability of rolling a 3?
    (b) Probability of rolling an odd number?

4. Parent tip

  • Play with real cups or boxes at home and hide one candy. Let your child play many rounds to ‚Äúfeel‚Äù about one-third.
  • Keep asking: ‚ÄúHow many good outcomes? How many total?‚Äù to build the habit.
  • No need for heavy terminology. Just repeat the idea of ‚Äúfavorable / total‚Äù.
Lesson 2 ¬∑ Drawing tool

Goal: Use tree diagrams to list all possibilities for multi-step processes.
Tree diagrams Multi-step Listing outcomes

1. Story: A frog in a tiny maze

A frog starts at the center of a tiny maze. At each step it can jump Left (L) or Right (R).

Lesson 3 ¬∑ Multiplication rule

Multiplication Rule ¬∑ Path Probabilities

Goal: Learn that the probability of a whole path (several steps) is the product of the step probabilities.
Path probability Independent steps

1. Story: Two-step walk home

Lynne walks home. At each corner she can go Up (U) or Right (R). Each direction is equally likely, so probability 1/2 for each.
She takes exactly 2 steps. What is the probability that she goes U then U again?

Step 1: P(U) = 1/2. Step 2: P(U) = 1/2 again.
For U then U, both have to happen:

P(U then U) = P(first U) √ó P(second U) = 1/2 √ó 1/2 = 1/4

2. Multiplication rule (kid-friendly)

If two steps are independent (the second step‚Äôs probability doesn‚Äôt change because of the first), then:

P(step A AND step B) = P(step A) √ó P(step B)

3. Try it

  1. You flip a fair coin twice.
    (a) Probability of HH?
    (b) Probability of HT?
  2. A box has 3 red and 3 blue candies. You draw one, eat it, and then the box is refilled so it‚Äôs always 3 red, 3 blue (with replacement).
    What is the probability that you get red twice in a row?
Lesson 4 ¬∑ Addition rule

Addition Rule ¬∑ All Ways to Succeed

Goal: Understand that if success can happen in several non-overlapping ways, we add their probabilities.
At least one Mutually exclusive events

1. Story: Win candy if you get at least one head

You flip a coin twice. If you get at least one Head (H), you win a candy.
What is the probability that you win?

List outcomes with a tree or directly:

HH ‚Üí win HT ‚Üí win TH ‚Üí win TT ‚Üí lose

There are 3 winning outcomes, each with probability 1/4, so:

P(win) = 1/4 + 1/4 + 1/4 = 3/4

2. Addition rule (kid-friendly)

If two events cannot happen at the same time (like ‚Äúthe outcome is HH‚Äù and ‚Äúthe outcome is HT‚Äù), then:

P(A or B or C) = P(A) + P(B) + P(C)

3. Try it

  1. Roll a fair die once.
    (a) Probability of getting 1 or 2?
    (b) Probability of getting 1, 3, or 5?
  2. Think: ‚ÄúAt least one head‚Äù = 1 ‚àí ‚Äúno heads at all‚Äù.
    Compute P(lose) = P(TT), then do 1 ‚àí P(lose). Do you get the same 3/4?
Lesson 5 ¬∑ Independent events

Independent Events ¬∑ Fresh Start Every Time

Goal: Know what ‚Äúindependent‚Äù means and see the difference from ‚Äúno replacement‚Äù situations.
Independence Coin flip model

1. Coin flips are independent

No matter how many times you flipped heads or tails before, the next flip still has probability 1/2 for heads and 1/2 for tails. Each flip does not remember the past.

2. Compare with drawing without replacement

  • With replacement: after drawing a ball, you put it back. The bag looks the same every time ‚Üí events are independent.
  • Without replacement: you don‚Äôt put it back. The color counts change ‚Üí later probabilities depend on earlier draws ‚Üí not independent.

3. Try it

  1. Flip a fair coin 3 times. What is the probability of HHT?
  2. Flip a fair coin 3 times. What is the probability that heads appears exactly once? (List all outcomes and count.)
Lesson 6 ¬∑ Conditional feeling

Conditional Thinking ¬∑ Choices with Memory

Goal: See how earlier results can change later probabilities, using ‚Äúno replacement‚Äù examples.
No replacement Condition changes probability

1. Story: Drawing cards without replacement

On the table are 3 cards: A, B, C. You draw one card and keep it, then draw a second card.
Question: What is the chance that the second card is A?

The answer depends on what happened in the first draw. That‚Äôs ‚Äúconditional‚Äù.

2. Two cases

  • Case 1: First card is A ‚Üí A is gone ‚Üí probability for ‚Äúsecond is A‚Äù = 0.
  • Case 2: First card is not A ‚Üí A is still among 2 remaining cards ‚Üí probability = 1/2.

If a child notices ‚Äúwe must split into cases‚Äù, they‚Äôre already doing great.

3. Try it

  1. A box has 2 red balls (R) and 1 blue ball (B). You draw 2 balls without replacement.
    (a) Draw the tree diagram. Put probabilities on each branch.
    (b) Find the probability that the two balls have different colors.
Lesson 7 ¬∑ Counting

Basic Counting ¬∑ Outfit Game

Goal: Use the multiplication principle to count combinations, a key skill for AMC 8.
Multiplication principle Combinations of choices

1. Story: What to wear today?

Lynne has 2 skirts (red, blue) and 3 T-shirts (white, black, pink).
Each school day she chooses 1 skirt and 1 T-shirt.
Question: How many different outfits can she make?

We can draw a simple table:

White Black Pink Red skirt R-W R-B R-P Blue skirt B-W B-B B-P Total = 2 √ó 3 = 6 outfits

2. Multiplication principle

If doing something takes 2 steps: first choose 1 out of a options, then 1 out of b options, and both choices are free, then:

Total different results = a √ó b

3. Try it

  1. There are 3 hats, 2 jackets, and 2 scarves. Each day you choose 1 of each. How many outfits?
  2. A code has 3 digits, and each digit can be 0‚Äì9. How many possible codes?
Lesson 8 ¬∑ Ball-box model

Ball-Box Problems ¬∑ AMC 8 Classic

Goal: Solve typical ‚Äúdraw balls from a bag‚Äù problems with tree diagrams and the multiplication + addition rules.
Drawing balls Without replacement AMC 8 classic

1. Story: White and black balls

A bag has 3 white balls (W) and 2 black balls (B), 5 in total.
You draw 2 balls without replacement.
Question: What is the probability of getting exactly one white and one black ball?

2. Tree diagram

First draw: W (3/5) ‚Üí second draw: W (2/4) B (2/4) B (2/5) ‚Üí second draw: W (3/4) B (1/4)

There are two paths with one white and one black:

  • W then B
  • B then W
P(W then B) = 3/5 √ó 2/4 = 6/20 P(B then W) = 2/5 √ó 3/4 = 6/20 Total = 6/20 + 6/20 = 12/20 = 3/5

3. Try it

  1. A bag has 4 red and 1 blue ball. You draw 2 without replacement.
    What is the probability of getting at least 1 blue ball?
  2. Hint: First find the probability of getting no blue balls, then do 1 minus that.
Lesson 9 ¬∑ State thinking

State Thinking ¬∑ Puppy Chasing a Ball

Goal: Build intuition for ‚Äústates‚Äù so multi-step random moves (like the cricket problem) feel natural.
State (Markov idea) Multi-step jumps

1. Story: Puppy running through rooms

There are 4 rooms: A, B, C, D. A puppy moves every step to a different room (never stays in the same room).
We care only about: ‚ÄúIs the puppy in room A?‚Äù or ‚Äúnot in A?‚Äù.

So we can group the world into two states:

  • In A: call its probability p.
  • Not in A (B, C, or D): call probability q.

2. Updating states

If the puppy is in A now, next step it must leave A ‚Üí next step is ‚Äúnot in A‚Äù.

If the puppy is not in A, there are 3 possible rooms all equally likely:

  • 1 of them is A ‚Üí probability 1/3 to be in A next step.
  • 2 of them are not A ‚Üí probability 2/3 to stay in ‚Äúnot A‚Äù.

This idea is the heart of the AMC 8 cricket problem.

Let p‚Çô = probability that after n moves the animal is in A; q‚Çô = probability it is not in A.

  1. Start at A: p‚ÇÄ = 1, q‚ÇÄ = 0. Compute p‚ÇÅ, q‚ÇÅ; then p‚ÇÇ, q‚ÇÇ.

Lesson 10 ¬∑ Expected value

Expected Value ¬∑ Long-Term Average

Goal: Meet the idea of expected value as the long-run average result of a random game.
Long-run average Expected value

1. Story: Candy mystery boxes

You can buy a mystery candy box.
  • 80% of the boxes have 1 normal candy worth 1 point.
  • 20% of the boxes have a big special candy worth 5 points.
Question: On average, how many points of candy do you get per box?

We can compute:

Expected value = 1 √ó 0.8 + 5 √ó 0.2 = 1.8 points per box

2. Expected value (kid-friendly)

Imagine you play this game many, many times. Add up all the points, then divide by how many games. That average is close to the expected value.

3. Try it

  1. Roll a fair die. What is the expected value of the number you roll? (Hint: average of 1,2,3,4,5,6.)
  2. Game: if you roll a 6, you get 3 points; otherwise you get 1 point. What is your expected score per roll?
